# Introduction

**Who Decides What Good Tutoring Looks Like? Culturally-Informed Evaluation of AI-Generated Educational Responses Across Diverse Institutional Contexts**

**Dr. Candice Idlebird — Claflin University — Sociology**

---

The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence into higher education has transformed how students access academic support. Tools powered by large language models such as ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude are increasingly deployed as tutoring systems, offering on-demand responses across disciplines ranging from the social sciences to STEM (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019; Brookings, 2026). Recent experimental evidence suggests that AI tutoring can match or exceed the effectiveness of in-class active learning under certain conditions (Scientific Reports, 2025). Yet as these systems scale across campuses, a foundational question has gone largely unanswered: who evaluates whether AI tutoring responses are effective, and does cultural position shape that evaluation?

Existing research has made important progress on adjacent problems. Scholars in natural language processing have documented the ways in which large language models encode and amplify cultural and social biases rooted in their training data (Blodgett et al., 2020; Bender et al., 2021). Sociologists have shown that technological systems — including search algorithms and predictive tools — reproduce racial inequity through design choices that appear neutral on their surface (Noble, 2018; Benjamin, 2019). In education, foundational work on culturally relevant and culturally sustaining pedagogy has established that effective teaching must affirm students' cultural identities and center their community knowledge (Ladson-Billings, 1995; Gay, 2018; Paris, 2012). Steele and Aronson (1995) further demonstrated that cultural context shapes how students experience and perform under evaluation conditions.

What has not been done is to bring these threads together in the context of AI tutoring evaluation. Prior studies have examined AI bias and tutoring effectiveness separately, but none has systematically investigated how evaluators from diverse regional and community contexts — particularly faculty and students at Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs) and other minority-serving institutions — differentially assess the quality of AI-generated educational responses. The cultural position of the evaluator has been largely absent from AI tutoring assessment research (OECD, 2024; Frontiers in Education, 2024).

This gap carries real institutional stakes. As AI tutoring tools are adopted at scale, the criteria used to judge their effectiveness will determine which tools get purchased, implemented, and trusted. If those criteria are developed without input from communities at HBCUs and other institutions serving historically underrepresented students, the field risks enshrining a culturally narrow definition of "good" tutoring (Generative AI Ethical Considerations, 2024). Equitable AI in education depends on whose standards count.

This study addresses that gap directly. Using a culturally-informed evaluation framework, we compare how diverse faculty and students across regional and community contexts assess AI-generated tutoring responses from three leading generative AI systems — ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude — across four subject areas: sociology, STEM, writing, and history. Drawing on mixed-effects regression and ANOVA, we test whether evaluator demographics — including institutional type, role, and region — produce statistically significant differences in evaluation scores, and we examine which models and subject areas show the strongest cultural divergence. The findings contribute to a growing body of work calling for equity-centered approaches to AI adoption in higher education, and offer empirically grounded criteria for institutions considering AI tutoring tools.
